Introduction

N'Ko is an alphabet used to write several West African languages belonging to the Manding dialect continuum (such as Mandinka, Bambara, and Dyula). It was invented in 1949 by a Guinean man named Soulemayne Kante in response to claims that African languages are inferior because they do not have their own writing[1]. The word N'Ko means "I say" in all Manding languages[2].

N'Ko is written right to left and consists of 27 letters + diacritics and digits. Letters within a word are connected by a common horizontal line at the bottom (as in ߒߞߏ).

Characters considered for the project

The script has been supported by Unicode since 2006[3]. A Wikipedia version is available. Currently there are three font families for the script: Google's Noto Sans N'Ko[4], Adobe's Kigelia N'Ko[5], and Tapiwanashe Garikayi's Afronik N'Ko[6].
